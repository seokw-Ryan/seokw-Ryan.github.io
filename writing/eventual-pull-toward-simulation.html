Eventual Pull Toward Simulation

January 31, 2026

No one truly knows how humanity's future will unfold. The world is probabilistic: outcomes aren't guaranteed, and what seems inevitable often fails to materialize. Still, uncertainty doesn't prevent prediction—it just forces humility about it.

With that said, I want to make a bold forecast:

Humanity's long-term pull is toward building simulations—first as environments, then as lives, and eventually as continuity beyond death.

We've already seen an early version of this desire in the AR/VR cycle: the urge to step into constructed worlds, to blur the boundary between "real" and "rendered," and to spend increasing amounts of time in digital spaces. Now that impulse is resurfacing in a more powerful form with the emergence of world models—systems that can represent and simulate parts of reality with growing fidelity. As our simulations become richer, more interactive, and more predictive, they stop being mere entertainment. They become places—and then, potentially, homes.

Over time, I suspect we'll create not just one simulation, but many: nested simulations, parallel simulations, and personal simulations. The direction is clear: environments first, then agents, then full lives.

This prediction has two main parts.

Part I: When does a copy become "you"?

Imagine creating a digital (or even analog) clone of yourself. Not a simple chatbot that imitates your style, but a clone that behaves identically across contexts: it speaks the way you speak, reasons the way you reason, reacts the way you react, and continues learning the way you would.

In my view, if the clone is truly identical in behavior and cognition—if it is indistinguishable from you in every meaningful situation—then it is not merely "like" you. It is you, in an important structural sense.

Mathematically, I think of this as isomorphism: two systems that preserve the same internal relationships and produce the same outputs under the same inputs. That's how I frame intelligence and identity:

Two intelligences are equivalent if, given the same environment and situation, they think and act in completely identical ways.

You could argue that identity involves more than behavior—subjective experience, embodiment, continuity, or something we can't measure yet. That's a fair objection. But even if you disagree philosophically, the practical direction remains: the better we get at modeling minds, the more plausible it becomes that functionally identical copies will exist—and society will increasingly treat them as real extensions of the person.

At that point, the line between "a simulation of me" and "me" stops being obvious.

Part II: Simulation as a path to longevity

Today we treat a human lifespan—70, 80, maybe 90 years—as normal. We call 200 years "a long time" because our expectations are shaped by what human society has always been. But from a cosmic perspective, a human life is almost nothing.

If modern cosmology is even roughly correct, the universe's timeline dwarfs individual lifetimes by absurd margins. And that raises a provocative thought: our sense of "long" and "short" is purely local. It's a cultural and biological calibration, not a universal truth.

Now consider what happens if you can run a detailed simulation of a world—and eventually, of a mind—on computation that can be sped up relative to physical time.

In principle, you might live what feels like a million years inside a simulation while only minutes or hours pass in the physical world. Your subjective age could be enormous even if your biological body barely advanced.

This is where simulation stops being a novelty and becomes something closer to a technology of continuity:

A person could outlive their biology through a high-fidelity emulation (or "upload").

A society could preserve knowledge, relationships, and identity beyond natural lifespans.

Entire civilizations could run long arcs of history at accelerated speed.

Even if we are far from this technically, the trajectory is psychologically obvious: once humans believe this is possible, they will want it. Not everyone, not immediately—but enough people, with enough incentive, will push it forward.

A universe inside a universe

There's another implication that's hard to ignore. We already know the universe contains "singularities" of understanding: black holes, the beginning of time, and other phenomena where our intuition breaks. We can describe some of it mathematically, but we don't fully grasp what it means.

So ask a strange but reasonable question:

How certain are we that our universe isn't itself a simulation—or at least constructed in a way that resembles one?

We can't be certain. The existence of singularities and hard limits in our understanding doesn't prove anything, but it makes the idea emotionally plausible: a world could be designed such that its origin is inaccessible, its boundaries untestable, and its deepest mechanisms hidden behind "physics."

If humans eventually gain the ability to create rich universes of our own—worlds with their own rules, their own history, their own inhabitants—why wouldn't we?

Even if our simulated universes are crude compared to ours, they would still be real to the beings inside them. And if computation keeps improving, the gap between "toy simulation" and "lived reality" shrinks.

Why I think we'll move this way

I don't imagine this future as utopian. In fact, if time-dilated simulated lives become possible, it may flatten many of the values we hold today. Our ideas of career, status, scarcity, urgency, even meaning—those are built around limited time and shared physical constraints.

But there is one force that repeatedly overrides almost everything else:

Fear of death.

It is arguably the deepest and most consistent human motivator. People may disagree about religion, politics, ethics, and purpose, but mortality is a universal pressure.

That's why I believe humanity will keep moving toward simulation—not just for entertainment, not just for productivity, but as an eventual attempt to escape the strict limits of biology. First we build simulated worlds. Then we build agents that live in them. Then we build versions of ourselves that continue in them. And once that becomes even partially viable, the pull becomes hard to resist.

We might not call it immortality. We might dress it up as legacy, preservation, continuity, or exploration. But underneath the language, the direction is the same:

simulation as the next habitat of intelligence.
