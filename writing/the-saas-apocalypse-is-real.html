The SaaS Apocalypse Is Real — But It's Not What You Think

March 28, 2026

The SaaS sector has taken a beating recently, and the reason being floated is straightforward: with AI coding tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and GitHub Copilot, anyone can now produce code at a fraction of the previous cost. The logic follows that the core value proposition of SaaS — abstracted, expensive software — starts to erode. And with it, those legendary profit margins.

I think that part is mostly right.

B2B SaaS companies in particular have enjoyed some of the most egregious pricing power in the history of technology. Unlike B2C, where competition naturally compresses margins, enterprise software vendors have been hiking prices almost unchecked for years. That era is ending. Companies will need to justify their price tags with a different argument now — not "look what our software can do," but "look what our software saves you versus hiring more engineers." That's a fundamentally weaker negotiating position, and margins will reflect it.

But Here's Where I Diverge

The same software engineers working at these SaaS companies are precisely the people who benefit most from AI tooling. They're the ones already living inside these systems, already fluent in the tools, already watching the wave build in real time. Their productivity won't just increase — it will compound. And that gives SaaS companies something most of their enterprise customers simply don't have: the organizational DNA to move fast.

Look at Perplexity. When it launched, it was an AI-powered search engine with a clear identity. Then OpenAI and Anthropic began folding search directly into their own products, and suddenly Perplexity's moat looked a lot more like a puddle. Rather than defending a shrinking position, they pivoted — hard — toward what they're now calling Perplexity Computer. That kind of pivot is only possible because their team was already deeply embedded in the AI wave. They felt the shift before most people could name it.

The New Moat

The same dynamic will play out across SaaS. Companies that aggressively adopt AI internally — not as a marketing line but as genuine operational infrastructure — will be able to reinvent their product surface faster than any of their enterprise customers can respond. A manufacturing company or a retail chain simply cannot move with the same velocity as a software-native business. That gap is the new moat.

So yes, profit margins will compress. That's almost certain. But the SaaS companies that deserve to survive will survive, and they may emerge leaner, faster, and more defensible than before.

The Compute Angle

There's one more angle worth noting: this is quietly a bullish case for compute. If the cost of falling behind is existential — and increasingly it is — then every enterprise, SaaS or otherwise, will be consuming more tokens, more inference, more AI infrastructure just to stay relevant. Compute demand won't slow down as AI gets cheaper. If anything, cheaper AI accelerates usage. The floor keeps rising.

Margins down. Compute up. And the fastest movers win.
