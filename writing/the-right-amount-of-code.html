The Right Amount of Code: Why More Is Not Better in the Age of AI

March 25, 2026

People need to be far more wary — and more conscious — about the explosion of token usage. It's not that higher token consumption is inherently better or worse. The beauty lies in using the right amount of tokens for everything: writing, testing, verifying, and exploring new ideas.

First, let's think about what an ideal codebase actually looks like — because many people are confusing quantity with quality. More code is not better code. That is the key insight here.

Yes, tools like Claude Code and Codex help tremendously in reaching a working codebase faster. But once you reach that point, you shouldn't be pushing as many PRs or commits. Instead, you should be spending those tokens to test new ideas, explore edge cases, and stress the boundaries of what you've built.

I do foresee an explosion in applications and web services thanks to the lower barrier to entry. But the real importance lies in the details. Can you design the system that performs the best? Can you debug — with AI — the problems that AI itself cannot few-shot solve? Can you design a product that is meaningfully different and better than what Claude Code or Codex first presented to you? Can you navigate a growing codebase and strike the right balance? Can you even imagine the ideal size of your codebase — a few million lines, or a few thousand?

In a world of easier code generation, the bottleneck will of course be you, because you cannot verify every single line. But it's also about accelerating yourself and understanding this balance. The lesson is simple: more is not better. As one wise man used to say — and I don't see why it should be any different in this era of coding.
